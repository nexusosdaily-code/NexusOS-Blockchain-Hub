## 1. Historical Foundations

### 1.1 The Quantum Revolution (Planck, 1900)

Max Planck's resolution of the black-body radiation problem introduced the quantum of action:

$$E = hf$$

Where:
- E = energy (Joules)
- h = Planck constant (6.62607015 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)
- f = frequency of oscillation (Hz)

This established that energy is not continuous but quantized, with each quantum carrying energy proportional to its oscillation frequency.

**Nobel Prize in Physics, 1918**

### 1.2 Mass-Energy Equivalence (Einstein, 1905)

Albert Einstein's special relativity revealed the fundamental equivalence of mass and energy:

$$E = mc^2$$

Where:
- E = energy (Joules)
- m = mass (kilograms)
- c = speed of light (299,792,458 m/s)

This demonstrated that mass is a form of energy, and energy has mass-equivalent properties.

**Nobel Prize in Physics, 1921** (photoelectric effect, applying E = hf)

### 1.3 Matter Waves (de Broglie, 1924)

Louis de Broglie extended wave-particle duality to all matter:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$$

Where:
- λ = wavelength
- h = Planck constant
- p = momentum

This proved that particles possess wave properties, and waves possess particle properties.

**Nobel Prize in Physics, 1929**

### 1.4 Electromagnetic Theory (Maxwell, 1865)

James Clerk Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism, showing light as oscillating electromagnetic fields:

$$c = f\lambda$$

Where frequency and wavelength are reciprocally related through the speed of light.

### 1.5 The Missing Synthesis

For over a century, these equations coexisted without explicit unification. Physicists used E = hf for quantum calculations and E = mc² for relativistic calculations, but rarely combined them to ask:

> **"If oscillation has energy, and energy has mass, what is the mass of oscillation?"**

$$hf = mc^2$$

$$\Lambda = \frac{h \cdot (c/\lambda)}{c^2} = \frac{h}{\lambda c}$$

$$\boxed{\Lambda = \frac{h}{\lambda c}}$$

The result is kilograms — a mass unit. The derivation is dimensionally consistent.
